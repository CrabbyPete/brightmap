Best Practices for Event Organizers

Lead Categories / Interests

  • Think of what your community buys or influences the purchase of
  • Think of the service providers that show up at your event already
  • Think of the people you already send referrals to
  • Think of the service provider types your community asks for referrals to

The best lead categories are for services that:

  1. People research the purchase online (or through community leaders such as businesses networking event organizers, where credibility/references are important)
  2. But when it comes time to buy, they usually go through a live person (rather than purchasing online)

So good lead-gen categories are things like: cars, houses, insurance, banks, schools, lawyers, accountants, web development agencies, hosting providers, etc

Bad lead-gen categories are things like: books/newspapers/magazines, bricks & mortar retail establishments such as bars/restaurants, anything you can find on Amazon, etc

Promoting your Lead-Buyers

  • Announce / feature them at the event (mention them from the stage/podium, sponsor logo in program slides/handouts, etc)
  • Write back to any responses from attendees who copy you

Integration with Meetup.com

  • Create meeting at meetup. Close off RSVP
  • Send an email to the list and direct people to Eventbrite directly.

Best Practices for Service Providers

Selecting your Lead Categories / Interests / Checkboxes

  • If your category is too broad, you'll get too many people who aren't really interested in your service
  • If your category is too narrow, you may turn away some people who otherwise are interested
  • Consider custom-designed interests for each community/event (e.g. 'web hosting' at ultra light and 'lamp hosting' at effective mysql)

Responding to Introductions (who/how/when/why)

  • Be sure to respond. If you don't respond at all, you won't get any clients. Recipients of these email intros rarely write back to the service provider unprompted
  • Speed is key. Ideally in 1 hour or less from the time you receive the email
  • Copy event organizer. So he knows you're actively responding and also to enhance the transfer of credibility from organizer to service provider
  • Use email tools to help research the lead (eg Raportive for Gmail or Xobni for Outlook)
  • Who responds?
    • If the product/service is technical, consider having an engineer or sales engineer respond if the leads are coming from a technical community
    • Don't send these to a generic email address (eg sales@mycompany.com). If you do, they are sure to get ignored. Send them to a real person, the person who is attending the event (or at least the person who knows the community).

Responding to Introductions (content - general)

  • Keep it short, 3-4 short sentences is about right
  • Keep it casual, phrases like "meet to chat" work better than "schedule a consultation"
  • Focus on the primary objective. The objective of your response email is to progress the lead to a conversation (either at the event or on the phone). Focus on this objective. Don't try to qualify the lead in the initial email (eg by asking if they have a budget, etc)

Responding to Introductions (content - specific)

  • Thank event organizer for the intro
  • Offer a meeting or call
  • Let them know if you're planning on attending the event, and if so, how to reach you
  • Your photo (particularly if you're attending the event)
  • Reference other business you've done in that community (eg other members you've worked with that they may know)
  • Provide some content such as:
    • Whitepaper
    • Checklist (eg "have you thought about x y, or z?)
    • Link to recent news in your industry or for your company
    • This is probably best as an attachment, so the text of the email is not too long
